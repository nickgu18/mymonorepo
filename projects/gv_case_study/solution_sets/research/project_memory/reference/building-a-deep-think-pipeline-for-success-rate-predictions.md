# Building a "Deep Think" Pipeline for Success Rate Predictions

 Large Language Models (LLMs) in their vanilla form can be **too naïve** for predicting success rates. By design, an LLM is a next-word predictor – it generates the most statistically likely continuation of a prompt, not a carefully reasoned or calculated answer. This means **LLMs often struggle with precise numeric predictions or probabilities**, tending to produce plausible-sounding numbers rather than accurate ones[reachcapital.com](https://www.reachcapital.com/2024/07/16/why-llms-are-bad-at-math-and-how-they-can-be-better/#:~:text=Viewing%20math%20like%20a%20language,questions%20may%20yield%20varied%20results). For example, given a scenario of a fair coin toss, an LLM might **over-confidently favor one outcome** due to learned textual biases instead of outputting a well-calibrated 50/50 probability[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.16007v2#:~:text=output%20proportionate%20probabilities,options%20in%20a%20calibrated%20manner). To overcome such limitations, we propose a **“deep think” style pipeline** inspired by recent advances (like Google’s *Gemini Deep Think* mode) to inject reasoning, structure, and domain knowledge into the prediction process. Below, we outline this pipeline and evaluate three key ideas for improving success rate predictions: (1) using a Mixture-of-Experts model, (2) using traditional models/architectures, and (3) implementing an iterative evaluation feedback loop.

 ## Deep-Reasoning Pipeline Concept

 In contrast to a single-pass LLM answer, a *deep reasoning pipeline* has the model engage in multi-step analysis and tool use before producing a prediction. Google DeepMind’s **Gemini** model demonstrated this with its **“Deep Think” mode**, which tackled hard math problems by exploring multiple reasoning paths in parallel and synthesizing a solution[gregrobison.medium.com](https://gregrobison.medium.com/from-silver-to-gold-an-in-depth-analysis-of-googles-gemini-deep-think-and-its-landmark-d3e07f9368cf#:~:text=At%20the%20heart%20of%20the,4%20This%20process%20is). The takeaway is that rather than trusting a one-shot LLM output, we can structure the prediction process into stages:

 -  **Chain-of-Thought Reasoning:** Have the model explicitly *“think step-by-step.”* For a success rate prediction, the LLM (or a smaller reasoning model) could first enumerate relevant factors, perform sub-calculations or logical checks, and only then output a final percentage. This guards against the model’s tendency to jump to a likely answer without justification.
-  **Tool Integration:** If numeric computation is needed, incorporate tools or deterministic modules. For example, the pipeline could use a Python calculator or domain-specific formula to compute parts of the outcome. Research shows that integrating LLMs with symbolic tools (like a Wolfram Alpha plugin) markedly improves accuracy on math-heavy tasks[reachcapital.com](https://www.reachcapital.com/2024/07/16/why-llms-are-bad-at-math-and-how-they-can-be-better/#:~:text=The%20second%20solution%20for%20improving,systems%20which%20yield%20higher%20accuracy)[reachcapital.com](https://www.reachcapital.com/2024/07/16/why-llms-are-bad-at-math-and-how-they-can-be-better/#:~:text=This%20can%20functionally%20operate%20like,spatial%20understanding%20and%20advanced%20algebra).
-  **Parallel Hypothesis Exploration:** For complex cases, instantiate multiple reasoning threads (analogous to Gemini’s parallel thinking). The model might consider different scenarios or methodologies for estimating success, then a controller module evaluates these and selects the most plausible result. This approach is inspired by how *Deep Think* could “explore and combine multiple possible solutions” simultaneously before converging[gregrobison.medium.com](https://gregrobison.medium.com/from-silver-to-gold-an-in-depth-analysis-of-googles-gemini-deep-think-and-its-landmark-d3e07f9368cf#:~:text=At%20the%20heart%20of%20the,4%20This%20process%20is). While full parallel reasoning is advanced, even a simplified version (like generating several candidate answers with different assumptions and then picking the best) can improve reliability.
-  **Incorporate Domain Knowledge:** Because this pipeline is intended for **vertical domains** (domain-specific use-cases), we should inject any domain expertise available. For instance, if predicting success rate of a medical treatment, the pipeline might reference medical guidelines or previously verified statistics as part of the reasoning. A domain-specific knowledge base or fine-tuned model can be queried in an intermediate step to ground the LLM’s predictions in facts.

 Overall, this pipeline mitigates the LLM’s *“next token”* blind spots by forcing structured reasoning and using external checks. It transforms the task from pure text prediction to a form of **inference process**, making success rate estimates more trustworthy. With this context, let’s evaluate the three proposed ideas in detail.

 ## Idea 1: Training a Mixture-of-Experts Model for Prediction

 **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** is an advanced architecture that could potentially increase prediction accuracy by partitioning the problem across specialized sub-models. In an MoE model, the network is split into many “expert” units, and a gating network activates only a few relevant experts for each input[medium.com](https://medium.com/@kanchan.iiestk/what-is-mixture-of-experts-moe-and-its-usage-in-llm-01810db7eee2#:~:text=1,to%20give%20the%20final%20output). In other words, instead of one giant model handling everything, you have a team of smaller expert models each specializing in certain patterns or domains, and the model learns to routelyour query to the appropriate expert(s)[medium.com](https://medium.com/@kanchan.iiestk/what-is-mixture-of-experts-moe-and-its-usage-in-llm-01810db7eee2#:~:text=,at%20solving%20the%20specific%20problem)[medium.com](https://medium.com/@kanchan.iiestk/what-is-mixture-of-experts-moe-and-its-usage-in-llm-01810db7eee2#:~:text=1,to%20give%20the%20final%20output). This yields two key benefits: (1) **specialization**, since each expert can focus on particular sub-tasks (e.g. one expert might handle numerical reasoning, another linguistic nuances), and (2) **efficiency**, since at inference time only a subset of the model activates, reducing computation[medium.com](https://medium.com/@kanchan.iiestk/what-is-mixture-of-experts-moe-and-its-usage-in-llm-01810db7eee2#:~:text=Results%3A).

 **Feasibility with available data – is MoE doable?** The MoE approach has proven effective in massive models like Google’s GLaM (1.2 trillion parameters with 64 experts) and Meta’s recent LLaMA 4 which uses 128 experts[medium.com](https://medium.com/@kanchan.iiestk/what-is-mixture-of-experts-moe-and-its-usage-in-llm-01810db7eee2#:~:text=LLaMa%204%20%28Meta%202025%29%20)[medium.com](https://medium.com/@kanchan.iiestk/what-is-mixture-of-experts-moe-and-its-usage-in-llm-01810db7eee2#:~:text=GLaM%20,2). These huge MoE models match or surpass dense GPT-scale performance while only using a fraction of parameters per query. *However, a crucial caveat is that MoEs typically require very large training datasets to realize their potential.* Each expert needs enough data to learn its niche. With limited data, a naive MoE might suffer from **expert under-utilization** – some experts might never get properly trained because the gating keeps picking a few dominant ones. Indeed, MoE training schemes include special load-balancing losses to ensure no expert “starves”[medium.com](https://medium.com/@kanchan.iiestk/what-is-mixture-of-experts-moe-and-its-usage-in-llm-01810db7eee2#:~:text=How%20to%20Train%20a%20Model,with%20MoE). If our dataset is small (say only a few thousand samples), a complex MoE with many experts might **overfit or collapse** to effectively a single expert being used most of the time.

 Given these considerations, **training a GPT-like MoE from scratch is likely not practical without massive data**. The original Switch Transformer (a type of MoE) was trained on billions of tokens to achieve its breakthrough results, and even then it required careful tuning of the gating mechanism. If we only have, for example, tens of thousands of data points for success/failure outcomes, a simpler approach may generalize better.

 That said, there are a couple of ways MoE ideas could still be leveraged:

 -  **Fine-tuning a Pre-trained MoE:** If a large MoE model pre-trained on broad data is available (e.g., **Mixtral 8×7B**, an open MoE variant of Mistral mentioned in literature[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.11574v1#:~:text=to%20evaluate%20X%20models%3A%20mixtral8x7b,and%20so%20on)), we could fine-tune it on our domain. This would give us the benefit of its broad knowledge and expert specializations, adapting those experts to our specific prediction task.
-  **Ensemble as MoE Lite:** We can emulate MoE benefits by training a few *smaller specialized models* (each an “expert” on some aspect or subset of data) and then combining their predictions. For example, one model could be trained solely on instances from Domain A and another on Domain B, or one expert could focus on numerical features while another focuses on textual context. A simple gating logic (even a rule-based or another classifier) could choose which model’s prediction to trust for a given input. This is a manual MoE in spirit, using traditional ensembling.
-  **Capacity vs Data trade-off:** If we do attempt a MoE, we might keep the number of experts low relative to data size. For instance, using 2–4 experts instead of dozens, to ensure each gets enough training examples. This smaller MoE might still help partition the problem (e.g., one expert focuses on cases where the input data indicates a high baseline success rate, another handles edge cases).

 In summary, **training a MoE similar to GPT is theoretically possible but likely overkill for limited data**. MoE shines in regimes where we have *abundant data and need extreme model scale*. With our constraints, the safer route might be to use MoE ideas in a limited way (fine-tuning or ensembling) rather than investing heavily in a new MoE training. It’s an idea worth exploring for scalability, but we must be cautious about the data requirements and complexity.

 *(Side note: Using an MoE doesn’t inherently solve the “LLM is bad at numbers” issue; it just gives more capacity. We’d still need to ensure at least one expert is good at quantitative reasoning, perhaps through its training data or architecture. Some research MoE models include specialized experts for arithmetic or logic.)*

 ## Idea 2: Using Traditional Models and Architectures

 Considering the data limitations, we should **not overlook traditional machine learning models**. Often, simpler models can outperform deep neural networks on small or structured datasets[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=The%20problem%3F%20On%20small%20datasets%2C,deliver%20stronger%2C%20more%20reliable%20results)[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=Why%20Small%20Datasets%20Change%20the,Game). The machine learning maxim *“no free lunch”* applies – the best model depends on the data at hand. If our success rate dataset isn’t huge, a complex model might just overfit noise, whereas a simpler model captures the essential signal.

 **Why traditional models?** Deep learning excels with *big data* (millions of examples); it can automatically discover intricate patterns given enough variety. But *on small datasets, deep models tend to memorize rather than generalize*[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=Deep%20learning%20thrives%20on%20massive,patterns%20instead%20of%20learning%20them). A recent analysis put it succinctly: *“Small dataset + deep learning = high overfitting risk. Small dataset + traditional ML = reliable, interpretable performance.”*[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=One%20carefully%20tuned%20traditional%20model%2C,model%20that%E2%80%99s%20starved%20for%20data). Traditional models like logistic regression, decision trees, or SVMs come with built-in regularization and make fewer assumptions about needing huge training regimes. They *“squeeze insight from limited samples”*, often delivering **stronger and more stable results** in data-scarce settings[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=memorizing%20patterns%20instead%20of%20learning,them)[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=The%20Strength%20of%20%E2%80%9CSimple%E2%80%9D%20Models).

 **Architecture options for success rate prediction:**

 -  **Logistic Regression or Linear Models:** If the task is to predict a binary success/failure (or a probability between 0 and 1), logistic regression is a solid baseline. It’s easy to train, **interpretable**, and works well with even a few hundred examples[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=We%20often%20underestimate%20how%20powerful,traditional%20models%20can%20be). We can include regularization (L1/L2) to prevent overfitting. Similarly, a linear regression (or Poisson regression, etc., depending on how success is quantified) could be used if predicting a continuous success metric.
-  **Tree-Based Models:** Decision trees and ensemble methods like Random Forests or Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost, LightGBM) are very powerful for many structured prediction tasks. They handle non-linear relationships and feature interactions automatically. Notably, **Random Forests perform strongly on “messy” real-world data without heavy tuning**[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=,classes%20are%20small%20but%20distinct). These models can also give feature importance insight (which factors are driving predictions), an added bonus for trust. On small-to-medium data sizes, boosting or random forests often beat deep nets because they don’t need as much data to learn splits.
-  **Support Vector Machines (SVMs):** SVMs with the appropriate kernel can excel in moderate-sized datasets, especially if the data is high-dimensional. An SVM could be used for classification (success/failure) or regression (with an SVR) to predict success probabilities. They are memory-intensive for very large data, but on a small scale they offer excellent control of overfitting via the kernel trick and margin tuning[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=,classes%20are%20small%20but%20distinct).
-  **Smaller Neural Networks:** If the data has many features or complex relationships, a modest neural network (e.g. a multi-layer perceptron with one or two hidden layers) might be tried. We would keep the network small to match the data size (e.g. a few dozen neurons per layer) and use techniques like early stopping and dropout. This can sometimes capture non-linear patterns that simpler models miss, without the overkill of a full LLM. It’s a middle ground when you suspect interactions that linear models can’t capture, but you have limited data.
-  **Domain-Specific Models:** *Vertical domain* context is key. If our success rate prediction pertains to a specific field, we should leverage existing models/heuristics from that field. For example, in credit scoring (financial domain), logistic regression with carefully engineered features has been a standard – we can start from such a known effective architecture. In a time-series context (say predicting success of events over time), maybe an ARIMA model or a small RNN could be relevant. The architecture should be chosen based on the nature of input data (tabular features, text, time-series, etc.). In many cases, a **feature-engineering approach** where we incorporate domain knowledge into input features for a simple model will outperform an end-to-end black-box approach on limited data. As the saying goes, *“Consistency beats complexity”* – a well-tuned simple model can be more reliable than a deep model that’s “hungry” for data it doesn’t have[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=Consistency%20Beats%20Complexity).

 In practice, we would likely **benchmark several of these traditional models** on our dataset. It’s cheap to train and evaluate them. Often, you’ll find that one of these baselines gives surprisingly strong performance. For instance, a Kaggle-esque approach might be: try logistic regression, random forest, and XGBoost with cross-validation. If one of them already achieves good accuracy (or calibration) on success rate prediction, that might suffice or at least set a performance floor that any complex LLM-based method must beat.

 Additionally, traditional models make it easier to incorporate **uncertainty and calibration**. We can calibrate predicted probabilities using techniques like Platt scaling or isotonic regression on top of a simpler model’s outputs to ensure the predicted success rates align with true frequencies – something that out-of-the-box LLMs do *not* reliably do[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.16007v2#:~:text=output%20proportionate%20probabilities,options%20in%20a%20calibrated%20manner).

 In summary, Idea 2 encourages us to **start simple**: use the right tool for our data’s scale[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=The%20truth%20is%2C%20performance%20rarely,the%20scale%20of%20your%20data). Not only might this yield better performance, it also builds confidence and transparency in the results[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=Building%20Trust%20With%20the%20Right,Choice). We can always increase model complexity later if needed (e.g., move to a deep model once data grows, as a phased approach[medium.com](https://medium.com/@shahzebmalik1085/performance-analysis-of-traditional-ml-models-vs-deep-learning-models-on-small-datasets-0a1dedc60356#:~:text=Start%20Small%2C%20Then%20Scale)), but it’s wise to exhaust the power of classical approaches first.

 ## Idea 3: Iterative Evaluation and Self-Improvement Pipeline

 The third idea is to build an **automated evaluation feedback loop** – essentially, a self-improving pipeline that learns from its mistakes over time. Instead of a one-and-done training, we continuously **evaluate the model/agent and use those results to guide further improvements**. This approach aligns with both human common practice (train -> evaluate -> refine) and emerging research on LLM-based AI agents that refine themselves iteratively[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=promising%20solution%20to%20automate%20this,stability%2C%20interpretability%2C%20and%20overall%20model)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=architecture%20selection%2C%20and%20hyperparameter%20tuning).

 Here’s how we can implement such a pipeline:

 -  **Establish a Strong Evaluation Set:** First, we need a reliable way to measure success of our predictions. This might be a hold-out dataset or cross-validation routine that simulates how well the model predicts success rates on new data. We’d track metrics like accuracy, calibration (e.g., calibration error if predicting probabilities), and any domain-specific criteria (for example, maybe false positives are worse than false negatives in our scenario, etc.).
-  **Identify Error Patterns:** After the initial model (from Idea 1 or 2) is built, run it on the eval set and analyze errors. Are there systematic biases? For instance, does the model consistently overestimate the success rate for a certain category of cases? Does the LLM agent fail when numbers get large? Error analysis might show, for example, that the model is poor at extrapolating beyond the range of training data, or that it misunderstands a particular input feature.
-  **Automate the Feedback Loop:** Using the insights from evaluation, we then adjust the model or pipeline. This can be manual (human-in-the-loop) or automated. A manual iteration might be: noticing the model’s weakness with numerical inputs, so you engineer a new feature (like a ratio or difference of two raw inputs) or add data augmentation (generate more examples of that pattern), then re-train and evaluate again. We can formalize this into an *automated pipeline* that repeatedly:
  
     1.  Trains or fine-tunes the model/LLM agent.
    2.  Evaluates on the test set.
    3.  Applies predetermined improvement strategies based on the results.
    4.  Loops back to training with those improvements.
  
   For example, if the model’s calibration is off, an automated step could apply a calibration algorithm or adjust the temperature of the LLM in generation. If certain subgroups have high error, the pipeline could trigger a data augmentation process for those subgroups.
-  **LLM-Agent Based Refinement:** A cutting-edge approach is to use an LLM itself to suggest model improvements – essentially an AI that tunes another AI. Recent research (like the **IMPROVE framework**) has LLM agents acting as a “Performance Analyst” that inspects training outcomes and recommends changes one component at a time[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=convergence%2C%20limiting%20their%20effectiveness,competition%20datasets%2C%20we%20demonstrate%20that)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=architecture%20selection%2C%20and%20hyperparameter%20tuning). The key here is **iterative refinement**: instead of overhauling the whole pipeline in one go, the agent makes incremental adjustments (e.g., “tweak the learning rate” or “try a different data preprocessing method for feature X”) and then re-evaluates[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=To%20address%20these%20limitations%2C%20we,Following%20this%20intuition)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=Iterative%20Refinement%20systematically%20updates%20one,identifying%20what%20drives%20performance%20gains). This mirrors how a human engineer would gradually tune a model, and it has been shown to improve stability and final performance over one-shot AutoML approaches[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=promising%20solution%20to%20automate%20this,stability%2C%20interpretability%2C%20and%20overall%20model)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=architecture%20selection%2C%20and%20hyperparameter%20tuning).
  
   In our context, we could have an LLM agent that, after each evaluation, reads the error report and *in natural language* decides on a tweak: *“It seems the model often mispredicts for scenarios with very low base success rate. I will try oversampling those cases in the next training iteration,”* for example. It could then execute that plan (possibly by generating code or configuration for the next training run). This kind of self-optimization loop is experimental, but prototypes have shown promising results in image classification tasks[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18530v1#:~:text=architecture%20selection%2C%20and%20hyperparameter%20tuning) and there’s every reason to believe it could help here if set up carefully.
-  **Continuous Learning and Deployment:** Beyond the lab setting, if this success prediction system is deployed, we can incorporate **online learning** from new data. As new outcomes are observed (e.g., you predicted a 70% success chance for a project and later see the project actually succeeded or failed), those can be fed back into the model. A simple version is periodically retraining on all accumulated data. A more ambitious version is live updating: some systems use techniques like reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) – in our case the “human feedback” could be the ground truth outcomes that eventually become known. Over time, the model should improve its calibration and accuracy as it sees more examples and learns from mistakes.

 **Ensuring Safety and Robustness:** One challenge with a self-improving loop is avoiding **drift or learning the wrong lessons**. We must monitor each iteration. For instance, DataGrid’s guide on self-improving agents emphasizes validating each feedback update to prevent cascade of errors (e.g., an agent could “learn” a flawed rule if a feedback signal is noisy)[datagrid.com](https://www.datagrid.com/blog/7-tips-build-self-improving-ai-agents-feedback-loops#:~:text=Companies%20face%20an%20impossible%20choice,data%20across%20your%20entire%20CRM)[datagrid.com](https://www.datagrid.com/blog/7-tips-build-self-improving-ai-agents-feedback-loops#:~:text=In%20this%20guide%2C%20we%20explore,without%20destroying%20your%20data%20workflows). Our pipeline should have checks – if a new iteration’s performance actually gets worse, revert that change. In other words, treat each automated change as an A/B test. This prevents the scenario where the agent optimizes for something easy but unhelpful (as the guide warned, an agent might “learn to avoid complex deals” to game its success metric[datagrid.com](https://www.datagrid.com/blog/7-tips-build-self-improving-ai-agents-feedback-loops#:~:text=Your%20AI%20agent%20just%20learned,was%20optimized%20for%20easier%20wins)). Careful design of the reward metrics and validation of improvements is necessary so that the model truly gets better in a meaningful way, not just overfits to the evaluation process.

 **Vertical domain adaptation:** Since we are focusing on vertical domains, the evaluation can also incorporate domain-specific *success criteria*. For example, in a medical domain, an iteration might include not just accuracy of prediction but whether the model respects medical safety thresholds. The improvement loop could then include multi-objective evaluation (e.g., maximize predictive accuracy while ensuring no predictions violate known domain constraints). By explicitly encoding these domain rules in the evaluation, the automated pipeline can adjust the model or its reasoning approach to align with expert expectations. Over iterations, this yields an agent that not only improves statistically, but also in practical domain competence.

 ## Conclusion

 To tackle the naive usage of LLMs for success rate prediction, we advocate a comprehensive approach that combines **structured reasoning, appropriate model choice, and continuous learning**. A “deep think” pipeline compels the model to justify and verify its predictions (mitigating the next-token randomness issue), while **Idea 1 (MoE)** provides a way to scale up model capacity if data allows, **Idea 2 (traditional models)** ensures we leverage simpler but effective techniques especially under data constraints, and **Idea 3 (iterative improvement)** sets up a framework to get better with time and feedback.

 In particular, recognizing the **limitations of LLMs with numbers** has guided us to include calculation tools or process-supervision training so the model doesn’t just guess, but works through the problem[reachcapital.com](https://www.reachcapital.com/2024/07/16/why-llms-are-bad-at-math-and-how-they-can-be-better/#:~:text=The%20first%20solution%20is%20reinforcing,supervised%20models). And by focusing on our specific domain, we can inject valuable prior knowledge at every stage – in model design (experts or features), in evaluation (domain-specific metrics), and in error analysis (knowing which mistakes are most critical to fix).

 By implementing these ideas, we move from a naive one-shot prediction to a robust, **expert-like system**. The end result should be a significantly higher success prediction accuracy (and confidence calibration) than what an off-the-shelf LLM could achieve. Just as importantly, this pipeline will be more **transparent and adaptable**: we can understand *why* it predicts a certain success rate and continuously refine its reasoning. This is crucial for building trust in the predictions – whether they are used for business decisions, medical prognoses, or any high-stakes domain – because stakeholders will rightfully ask not just *“what is the predicted success chance?”* but *“why should we believe it?”*. Our deep think pipeline, supported by the best of modern AI techniques and a strong evaluation loop, strives to answer both.
